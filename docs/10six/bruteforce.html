Brute Force History

 

2040

Formation of the Asian Economic Commonwealth highlights the reduction of insurgent conflict in the region. Eighteen member nations agree to a reduction of high-energy and conventional weapons, including overall numbers of ground troops.

Expatriation of many professional soldiers leads to the establishment of overnight military "cities" in Belarus, China, and India.

2041

World Court orders investigation into reports of arms deals and smuggling operations based in the expatriate military towns. Threats of embargo made against the "sponsor" nations.

2042

Brute Force Temporary Services founded in Manila. The company lists expertise in security, engineering, and general contract labor. Thousands of expatriate soldiers employed by year’s end.

First CEO is Richard Krager, a decorated British officer, member of the British Foreign Office in Moscow, and president of a private security and protection agency. Krager reputed to have connections with Russian underworld enforcer, Brutus Korov, reinforced in the naming of the company.

2045

Brute Force subsidiary Golysknaia awarded contract for Kepler II space telescope.

2048

Russian Republic contracts Brute Force to supply weaponry, vehicles, and advisory personnel in response to the Irkutsk Rebellion/Independence Movement.

2050

Partnership announced between Brute Force and Integrated Weapons Systems, a company long under scrutiny for suspected ties to LA, Chicago, and New York underworld organizations.

2052

Research and engineering partnership created with SciCon to work on a series of projects, including the multinational space station Beta.

2055

Worldwide employment tops one million.

After intensive investigation, admitted to the Economic Advisory Committee to the United Nations Global Economic Council.

2057

Sponsored by SciCon, Brute Force admitted to the Global Economic Council.

Richard Krager resigns as CEO. His replacement is Hahn Ng, a Singapore business magnate who trained in the Cambodian military.

2065

Brute Force consultants help design the rules and cybernetic gamesuits for eXtreme’s Virtual Combat. Proprietary armoring designs built into the flexibility of the suit.

2066

After gamesuits prototyped for Virtual Combat, eXtreme severs their contract with Brute Force. Contract law battle ensues.

2067

Contract law suit involving Virtual Combat decided in favor of eXtreme. Appeals by Brute Force lawyers are not reviewed by the US Superior Courts.

Brute Force contractors accused of attempting electronic forced entry and theft when eXtreme’s data security is compromised but not defeated.

Ng tenders her resignation under pressure from the Brute Force Board of Directors. Peter Kravstorsk, Russian defense industry veteran, takes the position of CEO.

2068

SciCon subcontracts with Brute Force to build orbiting Zgrav arena stations.

2070

Brute Force casts the decisive vote that keeps eXtreme off of the Global Economic Council.

2071

Brute Force accused of criminal collaboration with SciCon in the launch of a destructive payload at the planetoid Visitor. Brute Force disavows all knowledge of the contents of the missiles, demonstrates that their contract was limited to design and production of deep space launch vehicles, and is ultimately exonerated.

2072

Brute Force again under investigation for connections to the alleged sabotage of the Zgrav arena that killed eXtreme co-president Emil Perez. Investigation surrounds materials used in the thruster manifold.

Year long investigation spearheaded by ToyCo ultimately drops charges against Brute Force.

2073

Brute Force agrees to fund and provide contract labor in the joint GEC exploration of Visitor.
